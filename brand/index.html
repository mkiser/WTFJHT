The favicon is the brand: six rounded tiles — five crimson, one blue — on black. Black 71.5%, crimson #e0162b 18.4%, white, and exactly one blue tile (the W). Everything in this system derives from it: the crimson family, the single blue, the tile's corner geometry, black as a true brand ground.

Chapter 02

The seven laws

Law I — one blue
Blue #0052a5 appears only as the W-slot and as partisan-D data.

Never generic links, never info-semantics, never series-2 chart color. Red vs blue reads R vs D — that meaning is reserved.

Law II — the tile owns the rounding
The only border-radius in the system is the tile: radius = 0.21 × side.

Identity objects wear it (avatars, cover art, icon-only buttons). Everything else — buttons, inputs, cards, chips — is square. No pills.

Law III — one alarm
Crimson is the only color in editorial content, and it means links and alarm.

Dividers, decorations, and furniture are ink-free. The crimson rule exists in exactly one editorial fixture: the TIOS block. Instrument data colors are Chapter 19's domain — trend green/red with direction glyphs lives there, not here.

Law IV — charts survive grayscale
Every chart must read with color removed.

Lightness carries data; direction and category get glyphs (▴/▾) or labels, never hue alone.

Law V — flat ink
No shadows, no gradients, no hover-lifts.

State changes are color changes. Depth is for paper, not pixels.

Law VI — zero webfonts
No @font-face reaches the reader. Three system stacks, period.

The page is readable the instant it arrives. Vendor scripts that try to inject fonts get pre-empted (see Chapter 20).

Law VII — one box deep
A surface may group content; surfaces never nest.

Sections are marked by headings and hairline rules, not containers. A box inside a box means the outer box is wrong.

Chapter 03

The decision protocol

How every future design question gets answered — so decisions are defensible, repeatable, and never one-off.

steprule
1Map the question to a law, a token or scale stop, a type role, or a named canon element.
2If this book answers it — apply the answer and cite the rule. No taste votes, no invented values.
3If the book is silent or wrong — that is a book gap. Propose a principle, never a one-off. The owner ratifies the principle; the book gains the rule; only then does CSS change.
4Owner taste enters in exactly one place: ratifying principles into this book.
5Any CSS change ships in the same diff as its book update. This book is never stale.

Chapter 06

The crimson rule

The mark's crimson is OKLCH hue 24.8. Every red in the system holds that hue ±1° and varies only lightness and chroma. Edit reds in OKLCH, never HSL — lightening in HSL drifts the hue toward pink (the Abney effect), which is how brands go salmon.

The family, light to dark: #f6e9e8 → #ebccc9 → #ef8d88 → #e67a75 → #e0162b → #b50f22 → #96101d. Floor: never below hue 22 or above 27 — beyond that line lives the stock-palette coral this system retired.

Chapter 11

Leading, weights & the em law

Five leadings

tokenvaluewhere
display-tight1.12single-line display only
display1.18the wrapped headline
title1.25–1.3titles & headings
ui1.4meta, labels, nav, buttons
body / prose1.5 / 1.62sans body / Georgia

Tight leading belongs only to text that cannot wrap. Headlines take text-wrap: balance (no stranded last word) and body prose takes text-wrap: pretty (no orphans/widows) — progressive enhancement, 2026-06-06.

Three sans weights (+ Georgia 400/700)

weightdaynightcarries
400400400all body, both voices
600600600labels, nav, buttons
strong700600–700display, titles, headings, prose bold — night may step down one against halation, with scoped grayscale antialiasing

Retired: 300, 900, lighter, keyword bold (→700). Existing 500s map role-by-role: nav/labels → 600, body-ish copy → 400. The weight step: 700 belongs to display and title sizes, where bold has room; the heading role (1.2rem) and below carry 600 — at small sizes, bold counters close up. Inline microtype — code, abbr, blockquotes — is sanctioned relative grammar: it rides its prose context in em, because inheritance is the point.

The em law

The em law
Roles are rem. Em is licensed to exactly two patterns.

① data-inline — max(0.85em, 0.8rem): instrument text that rides its parent with a hard floor. ② post blockquotes (0.95em) — deliberately tracking prose. Em font-sizes never nest inside another em font-size. Spacing in em is fine and encouraged.

Chapter 13

The measure

38rem ÷ Georgia 1.1rem ≈ 63–71 characters per line — the calm centre of the classic readable band (tightened from 40rem, 2026-06-06). The prose column is load-bearing: widen it and the measure breaks. Any layout change re-runs this math before it ships.

Vertical rhythm hangs off the prose line box (≈31px): paragraph gaps 2em, story-item gaps 1.5em, section rules 2em. No baseline-grid theater.

Part V

Elements

Chapter 15

Links & focus

Prose links: crimson with a 40%-crimson underline, full color on hover. Tags and index links are underlined text that hover crimson — links speak link. On dark grounds links wear white with a soft white underline. Focus: 2px crimson outline on light, 2px white on dark, offset 2px — everywhere, no exceptions.

Chapter 16

Buttons

The CTA wears ink, not alarm. One primary button, square, with an inverting hover.

primary — ink square · #111 · hover INVERTS to white/ink (a real state, flat per Law V)
hover me
on dark grounds the ink square inverts at rest: white button, ink text, dimming hover

Action cards (membership packages): the whole card is a link to an action, so it takes the button's surface language — ink fill, square, hover lightens the fill — holding data-display content: white serif price, label-role caption. The featured package carries a 2px crimson border, the strongest emphasis pairing on ink. Cards hover by lightening, never inverting: a full surface flashing to white reads as a content panel, not a pressed control — the invert hover belongs to compact buttons. Never pills, never lifts, never shadows.

Chapter 19

Tables, numbers & charts

Instruments own their tables: mono caps column heads (label role), tabular figures (data role), hairline rows. Direction and category always carry a glyph — ▴14.1 ▾12.9 — because red and green are identical to colorblind readers (Law IV). Chart chrome: gridlines #aeb6bd (quiet, decorative), axes #64707a (must pass), data marks in the crimson family; blue only for partisan-D series (Law I). Every chart legible in grayscale.

The paragraph anchor

AnchorJS (the original library) provides hover anchors: placement left · visible on hover · truncate 36 · icon '§'. Two system rules make it conform: a pre-supplied style.anchorjs block stops the library from injecting its embedded icon font (Law VI holds — zero font-faces reach the page), and the glyph renders via a data-attribute so it can never leak into selected text or share-card exports. The anchor is furniture: always muted gray, never bold, never italic, never link-red, regardless of context.

Chapter 23

Search & archives

Chapter 24

The instruments

Big Grid, Big Board, Dashboard — data products in the same prose column as everything else. Consistency outranks grandeur.

One box deep throughout: section heading + hairline, flat hairline cards, no rails, no bands. The Grid's heatmap: crimson cells on #eceef0 empties (legible in grayscale), solid-crimson minimap viewport. The Board: tabular mono figures, direction glyphs, sparklines in quiet gray. Charts per Chapter 19. On dark instrument grounds, red meaning wears the dark-instrument red #e67a75 (the night-link value) — crimson #e0162b fails contrast on near-black and never sits there. Instrument numerals — counters, timers, stat readouts — are data displays, not text, and may exceed the type stops; their labels and captions hold the role scale. Two brand-chrome sizes are ratified off-scale: the masthead title and the author hero, both 2rem.

Chapter 25

The quiz

Plain stylesheet scoped under .quiz-container, outside the main build but inside the system: every --q-* value that mirrors a site token references it directly (var(--c-*, literal)), so the quiz cannot drift from this book. Square throughout, flat throughout, the one button spec with the invert hover, the prose column. Motion is color shift and fade — the score reveal rises, nothing scales. Serif questions at heading scale, crimson selection, success green only as machinery, mono keyboard hints. Every state — unanswered, correct, incorrect, results, retake — passes the contrast floors.

Chapter 26

Newsletter & platforms

Email maps the system to client-safe equivalents: Georgia prose 19px fixed, sans stacks for chrome, px sizes (no clamp, no custom properties), the same crimson and ink, single-column module width. The CSS-vanishes contract: when a platform strips styles (RSS readers, reader modes, Apple News), the semantic HTML must still read correctly — real headings, real lists, real decimals, no meaning carried by CSS alone. That contract is why TIOS pilcrows are real text and anchors are empty furniture.

Chapter 27

Print & night

Print is the fourth voice: Times at 11pt body with its own pt ladder (16/14/11/10/9), links underlined in hairline weight, chrome and anchors hidden, lists and quotes kept intact across page breaks. It is exempt from the screen scale and stays hand-tuned.

Night ships as a token swap (Ch. 05) plus exactly two type provisions: strong may step 700→600 against halation, and grayscale antialiasing applies under the night theme only. Sizes, leading, and layout never change by theme.

Chapter 31

Governance

How to add a color, a size, a width, a radius, a shadow: you don't.

New work picks from the roles, tokens, and stops in this book. If nothing fits, follow the decision protocol (Ch. 03): propose a principle, get it ratified, update the book, then ship — in that order, in one change. The gate enforces the laws mechanically — scripts/check_design_system.py scans every compiled build for off-system radii, shadows, weights, widths, sub-floor text, retired colors, and webfonts, against exact, documented whitelists. This book is the single source of truth; anything that contradicts it — including older reference documents — is superseded.
